From 0 to 1

A Fullstack Academy Retrospective

As I sit here a mere 40 hours after completing 17 of the most difficult weeks of my life, there is a slight smirk on my face re-reading a certain line on my Fullstack Academy certificate. This line in question credits me for completing the official 553 hours of web development course work, and I can’t help but scoff a bit as I think, “That’s totally NOT underselling the actual time I spent by an order of magnitude or two.” Don’t get me wrong, I am immensely proud of every single one of those hours on my certificate, but my mind refuses to forget the 3AM nights finishing workshops, coding on the subway, falling asleep and waking up to technical podcasts, dreams of my IDE screen, and not to mention the weekends and holidays spent working that are not directly reflected on my certificate.

However, this is by no means an attempt to be recognized for my after-hours work, nor is this an experience that is at all unique to me. Ask any of my fellow Fullstack alums and they will all tell you the same thing: every single one of those hours spent outside of class were not only expected in making the most out of class hours, but were essential in internalizing the most important lesson Fullstack had to offer. That lesson is not one based in code or technical prowess, it is not one that you will find explicitly in my code, but is instead foundational to my code and approach in general. Yes, Fullstack of course drilled into my cohort-mates and I with everything you would expect of a technical program, and yes, we all would likely talk about all of that before we mention it’s greatest underlying lesson. And yet, it is this almost mythical and subtle lesson that makes Fullstack or any quality bootcamp so unique and worthwhile as a learning institution.

But, like any good story, it wouldn’t make sense to make the reveal right from the start. Instead, I thought I would share my journey here in hopes that this lesson is abundantly apparent by the end.

The Calm: Foundations

This was my first official step into Fullstack Academy’s Fulltime Immersive program and certainly set the tone of what was to come. The Foundations portion was remote, allowing the flexibility to complete the material however I chose to between deadlines – but that was about the only slack it cut. Foundations got to the point almost immediately, introducing me to the UNIX file system and navigation through the CLI. Then, without skipping a beat, I was thrown into the depths of JavaScript programming. I was drilled and questioned about datatypes (e.g. arrays, strings, objects, null, undefined) and the JavaScript specific quirks to expect about them ( e.g. type coercion, equality).

The really fun stuff followed when we dove into the different variable types (e.g. const, let, var), their respective mutability/immutability, behaviors with respect to scope, hoisting, etc. Naturally we then dove deeper into functions, learning all about the Lexical Environment, scoping and closure, pure and impure functions. I must admit I struggled a lot with learning about closure and callback functions, and the introduction to declarative programming vs. imperative programming changed how I approached programming moving forward. But our foray into JavaScript functions would not have been complete without introducing us to Constructor functions and ES5 vs ES6+ ways of creating classes.

It was at the point where I reached the sections about the dreaded ‘this’ keyword, classes and functional programming that I truly began to see how much there was to learn about programming in general. Armed with the context I didn’t have before starting Foundations, I began to dedicate a much larger amount of my time outside of Foundations revisiting resources I found earlier to truly begin understanding what all of it meant.

I forgot to mention that each of the 4 weeks of Foundations included a checkpoint assessment that was mandatory to pass in order to continue the entire course. These checkpoints had practice problem sets that to this day still contend highly as some of the most stressful evenings of my life. These problems pulled no punches, and tested me on everything included in that week’s course… plus everything else up to that point. But it was in those practice problem sets where I honed the ‘out-of-the-box’ thinking needed to even attempt unfamiliar problems, and when to use what programming concepts most effectively. The problem sets that really stuck out in terms of time invested to understand was recursion and the call stack, both extremely technical and critical concepts that were key to not only understanding JavaScript, but programming in general.

Finally, at week 4, after spending so much time learning JavaScript specific syntax and pure programming fundamentals, I was given a taste of what JavaScript is actually used for in practical web development contexts. Here I was introduced to HTML and CSS, as well as the DOM and how JavaScript controls just about everything in the browser through the DOM. This was where a tangible shift in approaching JavaScript happened - after having spent so much time learning pure programming we took a sharp turn into practical JavaScript use which is admittedly a different beast entirely.

Practically teary-eyed with pride and having a laughably bare ‘Hangman’ and ‘Guessing Game’ under my belt at the end of Foundations, I finally felt comfortable calling myself a developer. Little did I know what challenges awaited me and how much my humility would be reset time and time again upon stepping on campus for the first time.

The Storm: Junior Phase

Every phase of Fullstack’s curriculum offered its own challenges, and where I would summarize Foundations’ as being exposed to coding deeply for the first time and mostly on your own, I would say Junior phase’s challenges were learning to code together and cranking the learning up to 11. Day in and day out, over the course of a full work day and work week, my cohort’s life became one with Fullstack’s mantra: “Always Be Coding”.

By the end of the first week, we not only recapped in greater depth concepts like scope, closure, prototypes, inheritance, first class functions, etc., but were also exposed to pair-programming for the first time. While at first the pair programming paradigm was new to virtually all of us and was certainly a clumsy learning experience, by the end of the first week we took everything we learned and recreated ‘Conway's Game of Life’. At the time, I remember clearly thinking that was certainly the most complex thing I would have ever attempted to build, dreading the day it came out of fear of failure. However, I will always hold ‘Game of Life’ as a shining example of success because not only did my partner and I complete it by the end of the day, but we did it TOGETHER where I don’t think it would have been possible individually. We learned how to construct 2-d matrices and apply that to an HTML table, how to simulate states and control over the board’s behavior through vanilla JavaScript and iron out edge cases and bugs.

The rest of Junior phase itself was a laundry list of other skill acquisitions. We covered the backend with Node, Express, SQL, Sequelize, Postgres, OAuth and Passport as well as the frontend with CSS, React, Redux, web sockets, HTML and everything in between. We learned data structures and algorithms, asynchronous functions, promises, testing, object-oriented and functional programming paradigms, the event loop and call stack, and how the internet works through protocols like TCP, HTTP/S. We built our own bash shells, promise libraries, CSS libraries, and trip planning apps. We cloned Wikipedia, Reddit, and Spotify across the stack, from creating databases to wrestling with React to finally understand state and props, and not to mention the ‘one state to rule them all’ with Redux.

While these skills and projects were certainly impressive, they gave rise to learning through extensive trial and error, effective debugging using the IDE or Chrome Dev Tools, and managing complex assignments by breaking them into smaller and thoughtfully interconnected pieces. I will always remember Junior phase as an incredibly humbling and frustrating experience centered around learning through intense struggle. Our workshops were designed with intentionally high difficulty in order to highlight any weaknesses in understanding. The Fullstack instructors always stressed understanding everything from ‘under the hood’ up through the stack over finishing workshops, and I slowly learned to strip my ego from my attempts at the workshops to expose everything I did not understand.

My nightly rituals became staying up incredibly late to finish and annotate every square inch of our workshops for the day, as well as reviewing the slides for the day, the next day, and all the pre-readings suggested to us. It was exhausting and intense, and many of us danced the fine line between learning and burn out, but this is where the basis of that mythical lesson I introduced at the beginning took form.

The Eye: Async Week

We were given 2 weeks in between our Junior and Senior phase to decompress and revisit the insane amount of knowledge we were exposed to during Junior phase. During the two weeks off, I focused on revisiting several workshops to refactor and optimize, such as rebuilding ‘Game of Life’ with React and Redux. Additionally, I met up with cohort-mates to begin practicing algorithms and more CS theory to hit the ground running for Senior phase. In between the algorithms and refactoring, I began to research ideas for a Hackathon projects and technologies I wanted to employ, and spent a full day building a custom ‘boiler-plate’ for future solo projects.

The Tsunami: Senior Phase

The 8 weeks of Senior phase were undoubtedly among the most high-stress, euphoric and grueling of my life. Senior phase was almost entirely comprised of projects, back to back and with virtually no hand holding. For the first 3 weeks, we were paired up in groups of 4, the largest number of people many of us had ever coded with, given a broad set of goals to meet in constructing an e-Commerce site, and were sent on our way.

For me, this was an introduction to team programming and took pair programming up several notches as my group and I had to learn to resolve conflicts and internal issues amongst ourselves to have any hopes of finishing. As was expected, the first week was a disaster as many hands were trying to work on many of the same things, and the general direction of the project was still very much in the air. As as we got better at implementing AGILE methods to our workflow and consistently checking up with each other, our project finally took some decent form a week in. I found the hardest part in all of this was managing and finding your role within a group setting and resisting the urge to just take control of everything. While all the technologies we used were ones we were familiar with, my entire group was learning to really design an entire web application from the ground up for the first time which was also a new challenge. Managing this group dynamic plus building an entire app from scratch was certainly the biggest learning experience up to that point. Feel free to check out our Pokémon purchasing app here!

Immediately after completing and presenting our e-commerce group project, groups were dissolved and as individuals were sent off on a 4-day coding sprint to build something entirely our own. No prompts, no directions, just 4 days. I took this opportunity to create NYSCENE., an application that renders hundreds of movie scene locations all around NYC and allows users to create an itinerary of locations to visit by saving them as favorites. For this I was forced to learn the Google Maps API and IMDb APIs, the first time I would be incorporating 3rd party APIs into anything I’d done. For the first time since I started coding, I was responsible for creating a fully functional application that did something, ANYTHING, and having full say over what technology to use and how to structure it.

The end of our Hackathon again marked a new beginning as we were regrouped into new groups of 4 for the most ambitious project of the program: Capstone. As a group, we were expected to not only come up with an idea, but then make it reality once again from the ground up. At this point the cohort was feeling the burn, having to start and stop projects only to build an entirely new one over again. However, this did not stop my group from dreaming big and stepping out of our comfort zone to work on something completely new to all of us: an offline desktop application for Postgres database interaction that added a layer of GraphQL to those databases for further data accessibility. With everything we had learned from our e-commerce group projects and Hackathon, my group was much more successful this time around in designing and delegating our project’s components. While the story of QLStico could be another post entirely, this project ended up being the single thing I am the proudest of coming out of Fullstack and the greatest learning experience of my life. From researching Electron to host our desktop application and learning Pg Node client to connect to Postgres as well as learning GraphQL to create and work with the schemas, the thousands of lines of code and hours of research resulted in a beta project that was even used by some of our peers in developing their own projects. Learn more and keep up with our continuing work on QLStico here!

The Horizon: Life After Fullstack

As you can probably already tell, there is plenty to say about the 17 weeks I spent at Fullstack Academy. While 17 weeks doesn’t sound like a long amount of time, dedicating every moment of that time has yielded me an understanding and skillset in software development beyond what I could have even though possible with twice that amount of time. It is because 17 weeks is not a great deal of time that it forced me to approach Fullstack differently and where I feel bootcamps in general shine. An instructor compared attending Fullstack to ‘drinking from a firehose’, being bombarded with new concepts and technologies to learn virtually every day, and surviving this challenge is the lesson I brought up from the start. The most salient learning I take away from my time at Fullstack is not one of React props or database design, but instead is the idea of learning how to learn.

Every day at Fullstack was an entirely new set of struggles to overcome in the forms of technologies I had never seen before. I had to learn and cultivate a habit of research to prepare for lessons a day or two before, to make failures a learning moment, to try and teach others to check if I really understood what I was even talking about. By the end of Fullstack, I realized how much more I sought out people in my cohort to fill in any knowledge gaps, and to take a moment of my time and explain to another who didn’t understand what was going on. Every single day was something new to learn, and it would have been impossible to make sense of even half the material without learning how to go out of my own way to ensure I understood everything, and to accept there is still so much more to learn. I accept I probably have only scratched the surface of what there is to know about web development and software engineering in general, but I feel that the skill I have that will propel me into success is I know how to learn what I need to.
